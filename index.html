RESPIRATION IN PLANTS

STOMATA

Stomata are minute apertures found in epidermis of leaves.

  • ‘Stoma’ refers to the stomatal opening and guard cells.
  • Each stoma is surrounded by two kidney-shaped guard cells.
  • Stomata are found in all aerial parts of plants.
  • They are not found in roots.
  • Surrounding epidermal cells are called accessory or subsidiary cells.
  • Guard cells are living and contain chloroplasts.
  • They contain more protoplasm than ordinary cells.
  • Dicot leaves have scattered stomata.
  • Monocot leaves have stomata in parallel rows.
  • Thousands to lakhs of stomata may be present per square cm.
  • More stomata are present on the lower leaf surface.
  • Some plants (like Banyan, Rubber) lack stomata on upper surface.
  • Floating plants have stomata only on upper surface.
  • Stomata open in daylight and close in darkness.
  • They help in transpiration (loss of water vapor).
  • A respiratory cavity exists below each stoma.

Opening and Closing Mechanism

  • Depends on sugar and starch in guard cells.
  • During day:
    • Photosynthesis produces sugar.
    • Sugar increases osmotic concentration.
    • Water enters guard cells → cells become turgid → stomata open.
  • During night:
    • Sugar converts into starch.
    • Osmotic concentration decreases.
    • Water leaves guard cells → cells become flaccid → stomata close.
  • pH changes control sugar-starch conversion:
    • Night: CO₂ increases → acidic → starch formation.
    • Day: CO₂ used → alkaline → sugar formation.

Role of Potassium (K⁺)

Influx of potassium ions into guard cells increases osmotic concentration, leading to stomatal opening. K⁺ ions move from nearby cells into guard cells.

Factors Affecting Stomatal Movement

  • Light:
    • Most plants open stomata in daylight.
    • CAM plants (Pineapple, Aloe) open at night.
  • Temperature:
    • Higher temperature increases opening (if water is sufficient).
    • Very low or very high temperatures reduce opening.
  • Water Availability:
    • Water shortage causes stomata to close.
    • This prevents water loss (Hydropassive control).
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂):
    • CO₂ concentration influences stomatal opening and closing.
